Difficulty Levels

Difficulty Level Dice Example
Simple - Dead simple tasks.
Easy
Picking a primitive lock, tending to minor cuts and bruises, finding food and shelter on a lush planet, shooting target at close range.
Average
Picking a typical lock, stitching up a small wound, finding food and shelter on a temperate planet, shooting target at medium range or trying to strike an engaged target.
Hard
Picking a complicated lock, setting broken bones or suturing large wounds, finding food and shelter on a rugged planet, shooting target at long range.
Daunting
Picking an exceptionally sophistacated lock, performing surgery or grafting implants, finding food and shelter on a barren planet, shooting target at extreme range.
Formidable
Picking a lock with no comprehiensible mechinism, cloning a new body, finding food and shelter on a planet without breathable atmosphere.

Navigation Hazard Setback Dice

Setback Dice Planetary & Space Description
- Flat, clear terrain. Roads, firm fields, grassy plains, or (if flying) clear skies and good weather.
An easy, relatively unchallenging navigation situation. A broad, loosely packed asteroid field, for example, or a thin, calm nebula.
Somewhat trickier terrain. Scattered trees, dense undergrowth, rolling hills, sand dunes, or windy weather.
A tricky but not seriously daunting set of obstacles. Flying over high mountains on a moon, or a thicker asteroid field or nebula.
Dagnerous terrain. A thick forest, steep, rock-covered hills, or flying in atmosphere during a violent storm.
A dangerous astronomical feature. Flying around or through a fracturing comet, or navigating through a particularly dense and turbulent asteroid field.
Very risky terrain. Sheer cliff faces, deep swamps, lava with only a thin crust of hardened rock, or speeding through a canyon only just wide enough for a vehicle to fit through. All are examples of flying or driving terrain.
An extremely dangerous situation calling on every ounce of a pilot's skill and coolness. Navigating the Maw, flying close to a deadly pulsar, flying through asteroid tunnels, or other dangerous and foolhardy pursuits.

Sublight Travel Times

Trip Time Example
5-15 minutes Time needed to travel from a planet's orbit to a safe hyperspace jump distance.
30-90 minutes Time needed to travel from a planet's surface to one of its moons.
6-12 hours Time needed to fly from one planet to another within the same star system. This time varies a great deal depending on the relative position of the planets in question, and any steller phenomena between them.
12-72 hours Time needed to fly from the center of a star system to its further limits. Again, this time can vary greatly depending on the stellar bodies and obsticles in the way.
